## Comprehensive Legal Summary

### Introduction
This is a Sessions Case No. 290 of 2020 tried by the Fast Track Special Court for Trial and Expeditious Disposal of Rape and POCSO Act Cases in Ranga Reddy District at Rajendranagar. The case involves the accused Koppu Raju, who is charged with offences under Sections 363, 366, 376(2), 354 of IPC and Section 3 r/w 4 of POCSO Act, 2012. The prosecution alleges kidnapping, abduction, and aggravated penetrative sexual assault committed against a minor victim from the same locality.

### Prosecution Plaintiffs Case
The prosecution alleged that the accused had developed a love affair with the victim and forcibly raped her in January 2018 when she was alone in her house. On July 18, 2018, the accused allegedly kidnapped the victim from her college, took her on a Honda Activa to Chandrayangutta, Nalgonda, Devarkonda, and Mahaboobnagar, and dropped her at Aramghar X road on July 22, 2018. The prosecution examined PW1 (father), PW2 (mother), PW3 (victim), PW4 (panch witness), PW5 (sister), PW6 (woman police officer), and PW7 and PW8 (investigation officers). They submitted exhibits including the FIR, 164 Cr.P.C statement, scene of offence panchanama, and the seized vehicle as Material Object 1.

### Defense Strategy
The accused Koppu Raju pleaded not guilty to the charges framed against him during the trial. During the course of the trial, the accused was examined under Section 313 Cr.P.C. regarding the incriminating material in the evidence of prosecution witnesses. The accused denied the said incriminating material and reported no defence evidence on his behalf. The defense counsel did not examine any witnesses or present any documentary evidence to counter the prosecution's allegations.

### Ratio Decidendi
The Court held that the prosecution failed to prove the guilt of the accused beyond reasonable doubt primarily because the victim (PW3), who is the key witness, was declared hostile and did not support the prosecution's case in court. The victim's version in the court contradicted her 164 Cr.P.C statement regarding sexual exploitation, and she stated she did not support the case of the prosecution before the court. The parents (PW1 and PW2) were circumstantial witnesses relying solely on the victim's information, and without the victim's support, their evidence could not establish the sexual assault. Furthermore, the investigation was flawed as the investigating officer failed to collect call data, did not examine neighbors, and there were corrections in the confession and recovery panchanama regarding dates and vehicle numbers. The absence of medical examination, panch witness for confession, and audio-video recording of the victim's statement further weakened the prosecution's case.

### Final Holding
The Court found the accused not guilty of the offences punishable under Sections 363, 366, 376(2), 354 of IPC and Section 3 r/w 4 of POCSO Act, 2012. The accused was acquitted under Section 235(1) Cr.P.C. for the above charges. The bail bonds of the accused shall stand cancelled after expiry of six months as required under Section 437-A Cr.P.C. The seized Honda Activa two wheeler bearing No. AP28 DL 7314 shall be returned to its owner after the expiry of appeal time on proper identification and acknowledgment. The unmarked case property shall be destroyed after the expiry of appeal time.

## Investigation Quality Audit

> **Lapse Severity Score: 8/10 (🟠 SEVERE)**
> **Rationale:** The investigation suffered from severe evidentiary gaps critical to a rape and kidnapping case. Key forensic evidence (medical officer not examined) and investigative tracking (CDR not collected) were missing. The chain of custody was compromised by panchanama corrections. These lapses fall under 'Severe - Fatal Evidentiary Gaps' as they directly impacted the ability to prove the core allegations of sexual assault and abduction, leading to acquittal.

### Police / Investigative Lapses
- **Lapse:** Failure to collect Call Data Records (CDR) of the accused's phone number.
  - **Impact:** Prevented tracking of the accused's movements and communication with the victim during the kidnapping period, weakening the timeline of abduction.
  - **Reasoning:** In kidnapping and abduction cases, CDRs are crucial to establish the accused's presence and movement. The IO admitted this failure during cross-examination.
- **Lapse:** Failure to examine neighbors at the scene of offence.
  - **Impact:** Missed opportunity to corroborate the kidnapping location and circumstances with independent witnesses, leaving the prosecution reliant solely on testimonial evidence.
  - **Reasoning:** Judicial observation noted the IO admitted not examining neighbors at the scene of offence, which is a standard investigative requirement for establishing the crime scene context.
- **Lapse:** Corrections in the confession panchanama regarding bike number and dates.
  - **Impact:** Suggests lack of attention to detail and potential tampering or memory lapse during recovery, casting doubt on the authenticity of the seizure panchanama.
  - **Reasoning:** Inconsistencies in panchanama documents regarding material facts (dates, vehicle numbers) are indicative of procedural negligence and can affect the chain of custody.
- **Lapse:** Failure to record victim's statement via Audio Video Recording (AVR) / Section 180 BNS.
  - **Impact:** Reduced the reliability of the victim's initial statement and deprived the court of a contemporaneous record of the testimony.
  - **Reasoning:** Recording statements of victims in serious offenses is a standard practice to ensure accuracy and prevent tampering. The absence of this is listed as a primary reason for acquittal.

### Forensic / Medical Lapses
- **Lapse:** Medical Officer not examined during trial.
  - **Impact:** Critical forensic evidence regarding injuries, medical history, or forensic findings from the rape/sexual assault was not presented or cross-examined.
  - **Reasoning:** In rape and POCSO cases, the medical report is a cornerstone of evidence. Failure to examine the medical officer renders the medical findings inadmissible as evidence.
- **Lapse:** No presentation of medical findings.
  - **Impact:** The court could not verify physical evidence of assault, making the prosecution's claim of rape uncorroborated by scientific proof.
  - **Reasoning:** The judgment explicitly states 'No presentation of medical findings' as a reason for acquittal, indicating a failure to utilize available forensic data.

### Prosecution / Legal Lapses
- **Lapse:** Failure to examine crucial material witnesses (Medical Officer, Panch witnesses for confession/recovery).
  - **Impact:** The prosecution failed to prove the chain of custody for seized items and the medical condition of the victim, leading to evidentiary gaps.
  - **Reasoning:** The judgment states the prosecution did not examine the medical officer or panch witnesses for confession and recovery, which are essential for validating the seizure and medical evidence.
- **Lapse:** Reliance on circumstantial evidence of parents without victim corroboration.
  - **Impact:** Parents' evidence was deemed circumstantial and based on the victim's information; without the victim's support, the case collapsed.
  - **Reasoning:** The court held that in the absence of support from the victim, the parents' evidence could not establish the sexual assault or kidnapping.
- **Lapse:** Failure to cross-examine victim's version effectively.
  - **Impact:** The victim turned hostile, and the prosecution failed to address the inconsistencies between her police statement and court testimony.
  - **Reasoning:** The victim (PW3) was declared hostile and denied the sexual assault allegations in court, and the prosecution failed to secure her testimony.

### Judicial Criticism
The Hon'ble Special Judge explicitly criticized the Investigating Officer (PW8) for admitting failure to collect Call Data Records and not examining neighbors at the scene of offence. The Judge also noted the prosecution failed to examine the medical officer, panch witnesses for confession and recovery, and that the victim's evidence was inconsistent and hostile, leading to the conclusion that the prosecution failed to prove the offence.

### Perfect Chain of Evidence
The chain of evidence was broken. The core testimony of the victim (PW3) was hostile and inconsistent with her initial statement. The medical officer was not examined, leaving no forensic corroboration of the alleged sexual assault. The Investigating Officer admitted to not collecting Call Data Records (CDR) and not examining neighbors at the scene of offence, failing to establish the timeline and location of the kidnapping. Additionally, the prosecution failed to examine panch witnesses for the recovery and confession, weakening the chain of custody for seized items. The parents' evidence was circumstantial and dependent on the victim's testimony, which was unavailable.
